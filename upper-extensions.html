Upper Extensions

Superimposing triads to imply advanced harmony

Larry Carlton built much of his signature sound on a deceptively simple idea: instead of outlining the chord directly, play a diatonic triad from a higher scale degree over it. Those triads naturally land on the 9th, 11th, and 13th — the color tones that give a chord its sophistication — while the rhythm section holds down the root and basic harmony below.

The result sounds advanced but the shapes are simple. You're playing three-note triads you already know, repositioned so they describe the extensions rather than the chord tones.

The G7 example

G7 is the dominant chord in the key of C — the chord that creates the most tension and most wants to resolve. G Mixolydian is the scale that lives over it: a major scale with a flatted 7th (G A B C D E F). The defining tones are the major 3rd (B) and the flat 7th (F) — the tritone that gives G7 its pull.

Over that G7 chord, Carlton targets two upper triads in particular:

The key insight

The rhythm section is already playing G, B, D, F. You don't need to. Playing Am or Dm in the upper register fills in the gaps — the extensions — and the ear hears the complete extended chord without you having to spell it out.

Going deeper: the 7ths view

Switch View → 7ths and select Am7. This gives you a four-note arpeggio: A, C, E, G — the 9th, 11th, 13th, and root of G7 all in one shape. This is the extended arpeggio version of the same idea: instead of a triad you're running the full upper-structure chord.

✦ The Lydian color

The natural 11th (F over Cmaj7) is an avoid note — it sits a half-step above the major 3rd (E) and creates a clash that muddies the chord. Lydian solves this by raising it a half-step to the #11 (F#), which floats above the chord rather than rubbing against it. That single raised note is what gives Lydian its signature "lifted," unresolved quality — the sound most associated with dreamy, cinematic major chord moments.

The upper structure that delivers this color is the D major triad over Cmaj7: D, F#, A — the 9th, #11, and 13th all at once. It's outside C Ionian (which has F natural), so you're borrowing from C Lydian. The rhythm section stays on Cmaj7; you float the D triad above it and the ear hears the full Lydian extension sound.

Practice tip

Start with the G7 → Am shape. Find it in three or four positions on the neck using the Triads view, then practice connecting those positions as a single phrase over a G7 vamp. Once that feels natural, add the Dm shape and practice moving between the two. You're building vocabulary that implies the full extended chord from the top down.

Modal Fretboard is designed to make this kind of relationship visible. Every diatonic triad is a potential upper structure. Every mode shows you which extensions are available.
